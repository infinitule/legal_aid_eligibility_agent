# Income ceilings for legal aid (Section 12(h))

> Reference note compiled from the Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987 and NALSA
> public information. Ceilings are indicative and are revised periodically —
> always verify the current limit with your State Legal Services Authority.

When a person does not fall into any of the categorical grounds in Section 12(a)–(g), eligibility turns on annual income. Section 12(h) sets an income ceiling; a person whose total annual income is at or below the ceiling qualifies for free legal services.

The ceiling is fixed separately by each State Government for cases within the State, and by the Central Government for cases before the Supreme Court through the Supreme Court Legal Services Committee (SCLSC). Because States revise these figures over time, the numbers below are indicative starter values only:

- Cases before the Supreme Court (SCLSC): a higher ceiling, of the order of Rs 9,00,000 per year.
- Delhi, Karnataka, Uttar Pradesh, West Bengal: lower state ceilings, of the order of Rs 1,00,000 per year.
- Rajasthan: of the order of Rs 1,50,000 per year.
- Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu: of the order of Rs 3,00,000 per year.
- A common default used when a specific State limit is not known: Rs 3,00,000 per year.

To prove income, applicants generally provide an income certificate, a BPL card or ration card, or a self-declaration/affidavit of income. The person only needs to establish that their income does not exceed the applicable ceiling. If the income is above the ceiling and no categorical ground applies, the person may still apply, and the authority can consider special circumstances.
